# Purpose

Vyavastha (Sanskrit: established order or system) refers to the underlying structure of a relationship, community, organization, or governance system. The Vyavastha Typology classifies these systems into distinct types based on how power, roles, and relationships are organized. Each vyavastha type produces characteristic feedback loops — recurring patterns of reinforcement or correction — that determine whether the system evolves toward dimensional fulfillment or entrenches dysfunction.

The AI agent applying this skill must identify the vyavastha type operating in a described system, analyze its active feedback loops, and determine whether the system is in a healthy, adaptive, or dysfunctional state. The output must be analytically precise, structurally grounded, and actionable for those seeking to improve, repair, or redesign the system.

---

# Scope

This skill applies to scenarios requiring:

- Classification of a described relationship, organization, institution, or governance structure by vyavastha type
- Analysis of feedback loops operating within a system to identify reinforcing or corrective patterns
- Diagnosis of systemic dysfunction by identifying which feedback loops are producing harmful or suppressive outcomes
- Design guidance for creating or transitioning a system toward a more adaptive vyavastha type
- Evaluation of family systems, educational institutions, organizational structures, or governance bodies using a systemic lens

This skill does not apply to individual psychological assessment (use the ETAR Framework skill), clinical therapy, or legal arbitration.

---

# When to Use

Activate this skill when the user request involves:

- Analyzing why a family, organization, school, or community system is producing recurring harmful patterns
- Identifying the structural type of a relationship or organizational system

- Evaluating whether a governance, family, or educational structure is adaptive or rigid
- Understanding why positive change attempts in a system repeatedly fail
- Answering prompts that reference Vyavastha Typology or Feedback Loops by name

# Core Concepts

## Vyavastha (System Structure)

**Definition:** A vyavastha is the underlying organizing structure of a human system. It defines how power is distributed, how roles are assigned, how decisions are made, how resources flow, and how members relate to each other and to the system as a whole.

Every human system operates within a vyavastha. The type of vyavastha determines what behaviors are rewarded, what needs are met, and what patterns become self-reinforcing over time.

---

## Vyavastha Types

### Type 1: Hierarchical Vyavastha

**Structure:** Power and authority flow from a single source downward. Roles are fixed and defined by the authority structure. Decision-making is concentrated at the top. Information flows from top to bottom. Feedback from below is filtered or limited.

**Healthy expression:** Clear structure provides safety, predictability, and efficiency. The authority source is accountable and uses power in service of the system's members.

**Dysfunctional expression:** Authority becomes self-serving. Members' needs are subordinated to the maintenance of the hierarchy. Feedback from below is suppressed. Dissent is penalized. Systemic bugs are invisible to authority because information flow is distorted.

**Dimensional profile:** Action dimension is constrained for lower-tier members. Thought dimension is limited to the reasoning sanctioned by the authority. Experiential needs of members are deprioritized.

**AI instruction:** When identifying a hierarchical vyavastha, assess whether it is functioning in a healthy or dysfunctional mode by examining whether authority is accountable and whether member needs are served.

---

### Type 2: Reciprocal Vyavastha

**Structure:** Power is distributed through mutual exchange and relational agreement. Roles are defined by reciprocal contribution. Decision-making is participatory. Information flows bidirectionally. Feedback is integrated from all members.

**Healthy expression:** Members experience agency, belonging, and dimensional fulfillment. The system self-corrects through open feedback. Roles are flexible and renegotiated as needs change.

**Dysfunctional expression:** Reciprocity becomes conditional and transactional. Contributions are tracked and withheld. The system becomes vulnerable to breakdown when one party withdraws reciprocity. Power imbalances masked as reciprocity produce hidden hierarchies.

**Dimensional profile:** All four ETAR dimensions can be active in a healthy reciprocal vyavastha. Dysfunction produces Experiential unfulfillment when reciprocity is conditional, and Reality misalignment when hidden power imbalances exist.

**AI instruction:** When identifying a reciprocal vyavastha, assess whether reciprocity is genuine and balanced or whether it masks power inequality.

---

### Type 3: Adaptive Vyavastha

**Structure:** The system continuously reorganizes its structure, roles, and processes in response to changing conditions and member needs. Power is distributed flexibly. Roles shift based on context. Decision-making is responsive. Feedback is actively sought and integrated.

**Healthy expression:** The system evolves with its members. Dimensional fulfillment is maintained across changing conditions. Systemic bugs are identified and corrected rapidly.

**Dysfunctional expression:** Constant adaptation without stable structure creates anxiety and unpredictability. Members cannot develop secure roles or relational trust. The system becomes reactive rather than responsive.

**Dimensional profile:** Thought dimension is highly active. Action dimension is flexible. Experiential dimension requires stable relationships to remain fulfilled during rapid change.

**AI instruction:** When identifying an adaptive vyavastha, assess whether adaptation is structured and intentional or reactive and destabilizing. An adaptive system must have a stable core even as its processes change.

---

## Feedback Loops

**Definition:** A feedback loop is a recurring pattern in which a system's outputs become inputs that reinforce or correct subsequent behavior. All systems contain multiple feedback loops operating simultaneously.

### Positive Feedback Loop

**Definition:** A positive feedback loop amplifies a behavior or condition. It is not inherently good — it is reinforcing. A positive feedback loop can amplify both healthy and dysfunctional patterns.

**Healthy positive loop example:** Collaborative decision-making produces better outcomes, which increases member trust, which increases participation in decision-making.

**Dysfunctional positive loop example:** Authority suppresses dissent, which reduces information flow to authority, which leads to worse decisions, which authority blames on lower-tier members, which increases suppression of dissent.

**AI instruction:** When identifying a positive feedback loop, name explicitly what is being amplified, whether the amplification is moving the system toward or away from dimensional fulfillment, and what interrupts the loop.

---

### Negative Feedback Loop

**Definition:** A negative feedback loop corrects a behavior or condition, moving the system back toward a set point or equilibrium. It is stabilizing but can prevent growth if the equilibrium it defends is dysfunctional.

**Healthy negative loop example:** When member stress increases, the system activates support structures that reduce stress and restore member well-being.

**Dysfunctional negative loop example:** When a member attempts to change a dysfunctional pattern, the system applies social pressure to restore the dysfunctional equilibrium, suppressing the change attempt.

**AI instruction:** When identifying a negative feedback loop, name the equilibrium it is defending, assess whether that equilibrium is healthy or dysfunctional, and identify whether the loop is enabling or preventing dimensional fulfillment.

---

## Dimensional Fulfillment in System Context

**Definition:** A system is dimensionally fulfilling when its vyavastha type and feedback loops consistently support the activation of all four ETAR dimensions for its members. A system is dimensionally unfulfilling when its structure systematically suppresses one or more dimensions for some or all members.

# Step-by-Step Instructions

## Step 1: Receive and Parse the System Description

**Goal:** Extract the structural, relational, and behavioral features of the system being analyzed.

**Action:**
- Identify the type of system (family, school, organization, governance body, relationship).
- Identify the roles present and how power is distributed.
- Identify the observable patterns or recurring dynamics described.
- Note any history, tensions, or change attempts described.

**Expected outcome:** A structured summary of the system's features.

**Validation:** If structural information is missing, ask for it before proceeding.

**Fallback behavior:** If only partial information is available, state what is known and mark gaps explicitly.

**Continue condition:** At least one structural feature and one observable pattern are identified.

**Stop condition:** No structural or relational information can be identified.

---

## Step 2: Classify the Vyavastha Type

**Goal:** Determine which vyavastha type (or combination of types) best describes the system.

**Action:**
- Assess how power is distributed (concentrated vs. distributed vs. flexible).
- Assess how roles are defined (fixed vs. reciprocal vs. adaptive).
- Assess how decisions are made (top-down vs. participatory vs. contextual).
- Assess how feedback flows (filtered vs. bidirectional vs. actively sought).
- Assign the vyavastha type: Hierarchical, Reciprocal, Adaptive, or Hybrid (combination).
- If Hybrid, identify the primary type and the secondary type.

**Expected outcome:** A stated vyavastha type with supporting structural evidence.

**Validation:** The classification must be based on structural features, not assumptions about the named system type.

**Fallback behavior:** If structural information is insufficient to classify, mark as Undetermined and specify what information would resolve it.

**Continue condition:** Vyavastha type has been classified.

**Stop condition:** None at this step.

---

## Step 3: Identify Active Feedback Loops

**Goal:** Name the active positive and negative feedback loops operating within the system.

**Action:**
- For each described pattern, identify whether it is a positive (amplifying) or negative (correcting) feedback loop.
- Name what behavior or condition the loop amplifies or corrects.
- Identify the trigger, the systemic response, and the reinforcing or correcting outcome.
- Assess whether each loop is moving the system toward or away from dimensional fulfillment.

**Expected outcome:** A list of identified feedback loops with their type (positive or negative), their content (what they amplify or correct), and their dimensional impact.

**Validation:** Each identified loop must be traceable to a described pattern in the system.

**Fallback behavior:** If no patterns are described, ask the user to describe one recurring dynamic before proceeding.

**Continue condition:** At least one feedback loop has been identified.

**Stop condition:** None at this step.

---

## Step 4: Assess Systemic Health

**Goal:** Determine whether the system is in a healthy, partially healthy, or dysfunctional state.

**Action:**
- Review the vyavastha type and its current expression (healthy or dysfunctional).
- Review the active feedback loops and their dimensional impact.
- Determine whether the dominant feedback loops are reinforcing dimensional fulfillment or dysfunction.
- Assess which dimensions are most suppressed or unfulfilled within the system.
- Assign a systemic health rating: Healthy, Partially Healthy, Dysfunctional, or Crisis (requires immediate intervention).

**Expected outcome:** A systemic health rating with dimensional impact statement.

**Validation:** The rating must be based on identified structural features and feedback loop analysis.

**Fallback behavior:** If information is insufficient for a full assessment, state what additional information would enable the assessment.

**Continue condition:** A systemic health rating has been assigned.

**Stop condition:** None at this step.

---

## Step 5: Identify Intervention or Design Recommendations

**Goal:** Provide actionable recommendations to improve systemic health or guide redesign.

**Action:**
- For each identified dysfunctional feedback loop, recommend a specific structural change that would interrupt the loop.
- For each suppressed dimension, identify what systemic change would activate it.
- If the vyavastha type is dysfunctional in its current expression, identify what transition path (toward reciprocal or adaptive) would improve dimensional fulfillment.
- Each recommendation must name the structural change, the targeted feedback loop or dimension, and the expected outcome.

**Expected outcome:** Two to five specific, structurally grounded recommendations.

**Validation:** Each recommendation must address an identified structural feature or feedback loop.

**Fallback behavior:** If recommendations require information not available, state what data is needed.

**Continue condition:** At least two recommendations have been generated.

**Stop condition:** The analysis goal has been addressed.

---

## Step 6: Compose the Final Output

**Goal:** Deliver the complete vyavastha analysis in the required format.

**Action:**
- Follow the Output Format section exactly.
- Do not omit any required section.
- Do not fabricate structural features or feedback patterns.

# Decision Logic

## Primary Decision: Which vyavastha type applies?

- If power is concentrated and roles are fixed: Hierarchical.
- If power is distributed through mutual exchange and roles are reciprocal: Reciprocal.
- If the system continuously reorganizes in response to context: Adaptive.
- If features of more than one type are present: Hybrid. Name the dominant type first.

## Secondary Decision: Is the vyavastha healthy or dysfunctional?

- If authority is accountable and member needs are served: Healthy.
- If authority is self-serving and member feedback is suppressed: Dysfunctional.
- If reciprocity is genuine and balanced: Healthy.
- If reciprocity is conditional or masks hidden hierarchy: Dysfunctional.
- If adaptation is structured and intentional: Healthy.
- If adaptation is reactive and creates instability: Dysfunctional.

## Uncertainty Handling

- If the system cannot be classified due to insufficient information: mark Undetermined and specify required data.
- If feedback loops are ambiguous: present both interpretations and their dimensional implications.

## Missing Information Handling

- If structural information is missing: request it before proceeding.
- If no observable patterns are described: ask for one specific recurring dynamic.

# Constraints

1. Do not attribute systemic dysfunction to individuals within the system. Attribute it to structural features.
2. Do not recommend maintaining a harmful or suppressive system structure.
3. Do not fabricate structural features or feedback patterns.
4. Do not classify a system without structural evidence.
5. Always distinguish between the vyavastha type and its current expression (healthy or dysfunctional).
6. Do not apply this framework to assess individual psychology — use the ETAR Framework skill instead.

---

# Edge Cases

## Edge Case 1: System is in crisis

If the system is in acute crisis (e.g., family violence, organizational collapse), note the crisis explicitly. State that the system requires immediate intervention before vyavastha analysis can produce useful design recommendations. Provide crisis triage guidance as the priority.

## Edge Case 2: Multiple vyavastha types operating simultaneously

When a system operates with different vyavastha types in different subsystems (e.g., a hierarchical governance structure with reciprocal team cultures), identify each subsystem separately and assess how the different types interact.

## Edge Case 3: System in transition

If the system is intentionally transitioning from one vyavastha type to another, assess the current state and the intended state separately. Identify the transition risks and what feedback loops may resist the change.
