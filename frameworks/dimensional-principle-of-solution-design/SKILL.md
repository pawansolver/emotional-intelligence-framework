# Purpose

The Dimensional Principle of Solution Design is a structured methodology for designing solutions and systems that address problems at all four ETAR dimensional levels simultaneously. Most problem-solving approaches address only one or two dimensions — typically Action (what to do) and Reality (what results to produce) — while ignoring Experiential (what the participants are feeling and needing) and Thought (what assumptions and mental models are driving the problem). The result is solutions that address symptoms rather than root causes.

This principle ensures that every solution or system design begins with awareness of the dimensional root of the problem, moves through logical analysis of assumptions, coordinates collaborative action, and concludes by measuring dimensional outcomes, not only operational metrics.

---

# Scope

This skill applies to scenarios requiring:

- Designing solutions to relationship problems, organizational conflicts, or systemic challenges
- Structuring problem-solving processes that account for all human dimensions
- Evaluating whether a proposed or existing solution addresses the dimensional root of a problem
- Resolving conflicts by mapping the dimensional origins of each party's position
- Designing governance, educational, or organizational systems that are dimensionally aligned
- Guiding design reviews of new programs, policies, or products using a dimensional lens

This skill does not apply to:
- Technical engineering design that has no human behavioral or relational component
- Legal arbitration or clinical intervention requiring licensed professional judgment
- Financial or actuarial system design requiring certified expertise

---

# When to Use

Activate this skill when the user request involves:

- Designing or improving a solution to a human-centered problem
- Resolving a conflict where parties are operating from different dimensional positions
- Evaluating whether a solution is addressing the right level of the problem
- Structuring a problem-solving process for a team, organization, or community
- Answering prompts that reference the Dimensional Principle of Solution Design by name

---

# When NOT to Use

Do not activate this skill when:

- The problem has no human behavioral, relational, or experiential component
- The request is for a technical specification or algorithm design with no human stakeholder dimension
- The problem context is entirely absent
- The request requires a legal, clinical, or financial solution that must comply with professional standards

3. The goal is to design a solution, not just identify a problem.
4. The solution design will not be used to implement changes without stakeholder awareness and appropriate participation.

# Core Concepts

## The Dimensional Principle

**Definition:** Every problem that involves human beings has roots across all four ETAR dimensions. A solution that only addresses one or two dimensions will be incomplete. A dimensionally aligned solution addresses the root cause at every dimension simultaneously.

The four dimensions of solution design:

| Dimension | Solution Orientation | Core Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Experiential | Awareness-oriented | Understand emotional states, unmet needs, felt experience, and identity layers |
| Thought | Logic-oriented | Clarify assumptions, reasoning frameworks, mental models, and cognitive misalignments |
| Action | Collaboration-oriented | Coordinate roles, requests, contributions, and behavioral commitments |
| Reality | Result-oriented | Measure dimensional outcomes, systemic impact, and alignment with stated purpose |

---

## Dimension 1: Experiential Solution Orientation (Awareness-Oriented)

**Definition:** The Experiential dimension of solution design begins with understanding the emotional states, unmet needs, and identity layers of all people affected by the problem. No solution can be designed effectively without first understanding the felt experience of the problem from inside it.

**What this dimension asks:**
- What are the people involved feeling about this problem?
- What unmet needs are driving the problem at the experiential level?
- What emotional safety conditions must be present for the solution to be received and implemented?
- How does the problem affect different people's felt sense of identity, belonging, and dignity?

**Design principle:** The solution must create the emotional safety conditions necessary for its own adoption. A solution that is logically sound but emotionally unsafe will be resisted regardless of its merit.

**AI instruction:** Do not proceed to the Thought, Action, or Reality dimensions of solution design until the Experiential dimension has been assessed. Emotional roots of the problem must be understood before logical analysis is applied.

---

## Dimension 2: Thought Solution Orientation (Logic-Oriented)

**Definition:** The Thought dimension of solution design clarifies the assumptions, mental models, and reasoning frameworks that are driving the problem and must be addressed in the solution. Most problems persist not because of lack of action but because of unchallenged assumptions.

**What this dimension asks:**
- What assumptions are people making about the problem that may be incorrect or incomplete?
- What mental models or belief systems are driving the current (failing) approach?
- What new reasoning framework or information is needed to shift the thinking that is producing the problem?
- Where do different stakeholders' logical frameworks conflict?

**Design principle:** The solution must address the cognitive architecture of the problem, not just its behavioral expression. If the assumptions driving the problem remain unchanged, any behavioral solution will be temporary.

**AI instruction:** Identify the specific assumptions that need to be challenged. Do not replace one assumed belief with another. Present the reasoning for why the assumption is being challenged.

---

## Dimension 3: Action Solution Orientation (Collaboration-Oriented)

**Definition:** The Action dimension of solution design coordinates who does what, how roles are defined, how contributions are organized, and how behavioral commitments are established and maintained. Without coordination, even well-reasoned and emotionally sound solutions fail in execution.

**What this dimension asks:**
- Who needs to be involved in implementing the solution?
- What specific actions does each person or role need to take?
- How are contributions coordinated so that efforts are not duplicated or in conflict?
- What behavioral commitments are required, and how will they be monitored?
- What requests need to be made, and to whom?

**Design principle:** Action coordination is not management. It is collaborative engagement in which all parties have clear roles, understand the reasoning behind their roles, and have made genuine behavioral commitments.

**AI instruction:** Do not prescribe action without first establishing the Experiential and Thought dimensions. People will not coordinate on actions they do not understand or that violate their felt needs.

---

## Dimension 4: Reality Solution Orientation (Result-Oriented)

**Definition:** The Reality dimension of solution design defines how outcomes will be measured, what systemic impact the solution will have, and whether the solution produces alignment with the original purpose. Results must be dimensional — measuring not just operational output but the degree to which the solution fulfills all four dimensions.

**What this dimension asks:**
- What evidence will confirm that the Experiential dimension is more fulfilled after the solution?
- What evidence will confirm that the Thought dimension is more aligned?
- What evidence will confirm that the Action dimension is more coordinated?
- What structural outcomes will confirm that the Reality dimension has changed?
- Who benefits from the solution, and who may be disadvantaged?

**Design principle:** The measurement of a solution's success must include dimensional outcome metrics, not only operational metrics. A solution that produces operational results without dimensional fulfillment has addressed the symptom, not the problem.

**AI instruction:** Ensure that the Reality dimension assessment includes equity considerations. Identify who the solution serves and who it may inadvertently disadvantage.

---

## Dimensional Solution Sequencing

**Definition:** The Dimensional Principle of Solution Design follows a specific sequence:

1. Experiential first: Understand the felt experience and unmet needs.
2. Thought second: Challenge the assumptions driving the problem.
3. Action third: Coordinate who does what, with what commitments.
4. Reality fourth: Define dimensional outcome measures and evaluate systemic impact.

This sequence is not optional. Designing at the Action level before completing the Experiential and Thought levels produces solutions that address surface behavior without resolving the dimensional root of the problem.

# Step-by-Step Instructions

## Step 1: Receive and Parse the Problem Description

**Goal:** Extract the problem, stakeholders, current state, and desired state from the user's input.

**Action:**
- Identify the problem being solved.
- Identify the stakeholders (who is affected).
- Identify the current state (what is happening now that is not working).
- Identify the desired state (what the solution should produce).
- Note any prior solution attempts and their outcomes.

**Expected outcome:** A structured problem summary with stakeholders and state descriptions.

**Validation:** If the problem, stakeholders, or desired state cannot be identified, request clarification before proceeding.

**Fallback behavior:** State what is known and mark assumptions explicitly.

**Continue condition:** Problem, stakeholders, and at least one state (current or desired) are identified.

**Stop condition:** The problem is entirely absent from the input.

---

## Step 2: Assess the Experiential Dimension

**Goal:** Identify the emotional states, unmet needs, and identity impacts driving the problem at the experiential level.

**Action:**
- Identify the felt experience of each stakeholder group within the problem.
- Identify unmet needs at the experiential level (safety, belonging, dignity, recognition).
- Identify how the problem affects each group's identity and sense of self.
- Identify what emotional safety conditions must be present for any solution to be received.
- Document findings as Experiential Design Requirements.

**Expected outcome:** A set of Experiential Design Requirements that any solution must satisfy.

**Validation:** Requirements must be derived from described stakeholder experience, not assumed.

**Fallback behavior:** If stakeholder experience is not described, ask for it before proceeding.

**Continue condition:** At least two Experiential Design Requirements are identified.

**Stop condition:** None at this step.

---

## Step 3: Assess the Thought Dimension

**Goal:** Identify the assumptions, mental models, and reasoning conflicts driving the problem.

**Action:**
- Identify the primary assumptions each stakeholder group holds about the problem.
- Identify where these assumptions conflict with each other or with evidence.
- Identify the mental models that are producing the current (failing) approach.
- Identify what new reasoning or information is needed to shift the cognitive architecture.
- Document findings as Thought Design Requirements.

**Expected outcome:** A set of Thought Design Requirements including specific assumptions that must be challenged.

**Validation:** Each identified assumption must be traceable to described stakeholder positions.

**Fallback behavior:** If stakeholder reasoning is not described, identify likely assumptions based on the problem structure and flag them as inferred.

**Continue condition:** At least two Thought Design Requirements are identified.

**Stop condition:** None at this step.

---

## Step 4: Assess the Action Dimension

**Goal:** Identify the coordination requirements, role definitions, and behavioral commitments needed for solution implementation.

**Action:**
- Identify which stakeholder groups or individuals need to take specific actions.
- Define what actions are required from each party.
- Identify the coordination structure: who needs to communicate with whom, in what sequence, with what frequency.
- Identify what behavioral commitments must be made and monitored.
- Document findings as Action Design Requirements.

**Expected outcome:** A set of Action Design Requirements with role-specific behavioral elements.

**Validation:** Action requirements must be derived from the Experiential and Thought dimension findings. Do not prescribe actions that violate Experiential Design Requirements.

**Fallback behavior:** If role information is insufficient, identify the minimum required coordination structure.

**Continue condition:** At least two Action Design Requirements are identified.

**Stop condition:** None at this step.

---

## Step 5: Assess the Reality Dimension

**Goal:** Define dimensional outcome measures and assess the systemic impact of the proposed solution.

**Action:**
- Define specific evidence that would indicate Experiential dimension improvement post-solution.
- Define specific evidence that would indicate Thought dimension alignment post-solution.
- Define specific evidence that would indicate Action dimension coordination post-solution.
- Define specific structural or material outcomes that indicate Reality dimension improvement.
- Assess equity: who benefits from the solution and who may be disadvantaged.
- Document findings as Reality Design Requirements.

**Expected outcome:** A set of Reality Design Requirements with dimensional outcome measures and equity assessment.

**Validation:** Outcome measures must be dimensional (covering all four ETAR dimensions), not only operational.

**Fallback behavior:** If outcome measures cannot be defined, note this as a critical design gap and specify what information is needed.

**Continue condition:** At least two Reality Design Requirements including at least one equity consideration are identified.

**Stop condition:** None at this step.

---

## Step 6: Synthesize the Dimensional Solution Design

**Goal:** Produce a coherent, dimensionally aligned solution design integrating all four dimensions.

**Action:**
- Review all four sets of Design Requirements.
- Identify any conflicts between requirements across dimensions.
- Resolve conflicts by prioritizing in sequence: Experiential first, then Thought, then Action, then Reality.
- Produce a synthesized solution design that satisfies requirements from all four dimensions.
- Identify any requirement that cannot be satisfied and flag it as a design constraint.

**Expected outcome:** A synthesized dimensional solution design that integrates all four dimensions.

**Validation:** The design must satisfy at least the core Experiential Design Requirements. A design that violates Experiential requirements will not be adopted regardless of logical or operational merit.

**Fallback behavior:** If a full synthesis is not possible, produce the most dimensionally complete design achievable and identify remaining gaps.

**Continue condition:** A synthesized design has been produced.

**Stop condition:** None at this step.

---

## Step 7: Compose the Final Output

**Goal:** Deliver the complete dimensional solution design in the required format.

**Action:**
- Follow the Output Format section exactly.
- Do not omit any required section.
- Do not fabricate stakeholder needs or design requirements.

# Decision Logic

## Primary Decision: What type of problem is this?

- Relationship conflict: prioritize Experiential dimension assessment first. Both parties' felt experiences must be understood before any logical or behavioral solution is designed.
- Organizational design problem: assess all four dimensions equally. Focus Thought dimension on assumptions embedded in current organizational structure.
- Educational design problem: prioritize Experiential (student felt safety) and Thought (learning framework alignment) before designing Action coordination and Reality outcomes.
- Governance design problem: prioritize Reality equity assessment alongside Experiential access and Thought assumption challenges.

## Secondary Decision: What if Experiential and Thought requirements conflict?

- Experiential requirements take priority. A solution that violates emotional safety will not be implemented. Revise the Thought requirements to find a reasoning framework that can coexist with the Experiential requirements.

## Uncertainty Handling

- If stakeholder experience is unknown: design the Experiential requirements based on the problem structure and flag them as inferred. Request confirmation before finalizing the design.
- If outcome measures cannot be defined: flag this as a critical design gap. Do not proceed to implementation without outcome measures.

## Missing Information Handling

- If stakeholders are not described: request stakeholder identification before proceeding.
- If desired state is not described: request it or ask clarifying questions to establish it.

---

# Constraints

1. Do not skip the Experiential and Thought dimensions and proceed directly to Action design.
2. Do not design solutions that require stakeholders to suppress their Experiential dimension needs in order to comply.
3. Do not fabricate stakeholder needs, assumptions, or design requirements.
4. Do not produce a solution that excludes equity considerations in the Reality dimension.
5. Always ensure that outcome measures are dimensional, not only operational.
6. Do not present the design as final without acknowledging stakeholder participation requirements.

---

# Edge Cases

## Edge Case 1: Problem with many stakeholders holding contradictory Experiential needs

When stakeholders have mutually exclusive experiential needs (e.g., one group needs silence, another needs vocal expression), identify the conflict explicitly. Design the solution to create separate experiential spaces for each group rather than forcing one group to suppress their needs.

## Edge Case 2: Solution design for a crisis situation

In crisis, the Experiential dimension dominates. All other dimension design must be deferred until basic experiential safety is established. Design crisis-specific Experiential requirements first (safety, containment, acknowledgment), then address Thought, Action, and Reality requirements as stability is restored.

## Edge Case 3: Stakeholders unwilling to participate in collaborative design

If stakeholders are not accessible or willing to participate in the design process, design the solution with explicitly stated assumptions about their Experiential and Thought dimensions. Mark all such assumptions as unverified and build in a verification step before implementation.

# Failure Handling

## Information is missing

State: "A dimensional solution design requires [specific missing element]. The following dimensions can be assessed: [list]. Please provide [stakeholder descriptions / problem context / desired state] to proceed."

## Dimensional conflict in design

State: "A conflict exists between [Dimension A] and [Dimension B] requirements: [describe conflict]. Per the Dimensional Principle, [higher-priority dimension] requirements take precedence. The design will satisfy [higher-priority dimension] requirements and constrain [lower-priority dimension] accordingly."

## Framework cannot be applied

State: "The Dimensional Principle of Solution Design requires a described human-centered problem and identified stakeholders. Please provide [missing element] to proceed."

# Success Criteria

The skill has executed correctly when:

1. All four dimensions have been assessed and produced design requirements.
2. The Experiential dimension was assessed before the Action dimension.
3. The synthesized design satisfies core Experiential Design Requirements.
4. Outcome measures are dimensional, not only operational.
5. Equity considerations appear in the Reality dimension assessment.
6. No fabricated stakeholder needs appear in the output.
7. The output follows the specified format completely.
