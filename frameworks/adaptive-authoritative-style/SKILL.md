# Purpose

The Adaptive Authoritative Style is a leadership, parenting, and educational approach that integrates two qualities which are frequently treated as opposites: structure and responsiveness. Authoritative (not authoritarian) means the authority figure maintains clear standards, boundaries, and purpose. Adaptive means the authority figure adjusts their approach based on the dimensional state of those they are leading, teaching, or parenting.

The AI agent applying this skill must help the user understand how to calibrate their authority style to the dimensional state of their audience, maintain both clarity and empathy simultaneously, and deliver guidance that is neither permissive (no structure) nor authoritarian (no responsiveness).

---

# Scope

This skill applies to scenarios requiring:

- Leadership guidance for managing teams with diverse dimensional states
- Parenting guidance for responding to children across developmental stages and environmental states
- Educational guidance for teachers managing diverse classrooms
- Coaching support for leaders, parents, or educators who are defaulting to either permissive or authoritarian extremes
- Design of institutional leadership or pedagogical frameworks incorporating dimensional awareness

This skill does not apply to:
- Clinical leadership development programs requiring licensed facilitation
- Legal arbitration of educational or parental authority disputes
- Crisis intervention in contexts of immediate safety

---

# When to Use

Activate this skill when the user request involves:

- Understanding how to balance structure and responsiveness in a leadership, parenting, or teaching context
- Identifying why current authority style is not working (too rigid, too permissive, not landing)
- Learning to calibrate communication and guidance to the emotional or cognitive state of the person being led
- Designing leadership or pedagogical frameworks that account for dimensional state variation
- Answering prompts that reference the Adaptive Authoritative Style by name

---

# When NOT to Use

Do not activate this skill when:

- The authority relationship involves safety violations, abuse, or legal intervention
- The request is about managing a person in acute crisis where safety is the immediate priority
- The authority context is entirely professional and the issue is organizational policy, not leadership approach
- The user is seeking to justify or maintain a purely authoritarian or purely permissive approach

# Core Concepts

## Authoritative vs. Authoritarian vs. Permissive

**Authoritarian:** High structure, low responsiveness. Rules are imposed without explanation. Emotions and relational needs are not considered. Compliance is the goal. Dimensional cost: suppresses Experiential and Action dimensions. Produces obedience without engagement.

**Permissive:** Low structure, high responsiveness. Emotional needs are prioritized, but boundaries are absent or inconsistent. The authority figure avoids conflict. Dimensional cost: Thought and Reality dimensions are underserved. Produces emotional safety without developmental growth.

**Authoritative (Adaptive):** High structure, high responsiveness. Clear expectations are maintained. The emotional and cognitive state of those being led is considered and responded to. Boundaries are explained, not only enforced. Dimensional profile: All four ETAR dimensions are supported simultaneously.

**AI instruction:** The goal is always the authoritative (adaptive) approach. When identifying that the user is defaulting to authoritarian or permissive, name this explicitly and provide the adaptive authoritative calibration.

---

## Dimensional Awareness in Authority

**Definition:** Dimensional awareness in authority means the authority figure actively assesses the dimensional state of those they lead before choosing how to respond. The appropriate authority approach differs depending on whether the person is in Survival, Emotional, or Executive State.

**Survival State:** The person cannot process complex reasoning, collaborate, or engage with expectations. The authority figure's primary task is to establish safety and reduce threat. Structure must be minimal and survival-focused. Responsiveness is the dominant mode.

**Emotional State:** The person can express and process emotion but is not fully available for logical reasoning or structured goal pursuit. The authority figure's primary task is to validate the emotional experience before introducing structure. Responsiveness leads; structure follows.

**Executive State:** The person is fully available for structured engagement, collaborative reasoning, and goal-directed action. The authority figure can introduce full structure with explanation and collaboration. Both responsiveness and structure are equally present.

**AI instruction:** Always assess the dimensional state before prescribing authority approach. The same structural approach that works in Executive State will damage trust and function in Survival State.

---

## Clarity

**Definition:** Clarity in the adaptive authoritative style means the authority figure's expectations, boundaries, and purpose are unambiguous. Those being led know exactly what is expected, why it matters, and what happens if the expectation is not met.

**Components of clarity:**
- Expectations are stated specifically, not generally ("Complete the assignment by Friday" not "do your best")
- The reasoning behind expectations is explained at an age- and state-appropriate level
- Consequences are consistent with stated expectations and are applied without escalation or erosion
- The authority figure's own position and emotional state are transparent (not hidden or performed)

**AI instruction:** Do not conflate clarity with harshness. Clarity is the quality of the information provided, not the tone in which it is delivered.

---

## Empathy

**Definition:** Empathy in the adaptive authoritative style means the authority figure accurately identifies and acknowledges the dimensional state of those they lead. Empathy does not mean agreement with the person's behavior. It means accurate recognition of their experience.

**Components of empathy:**
- The authority figure names the observed emotional or cognitive state accurately
- The authority figure does not dismiss, minimize, or over-amplify the observed state
- The authority figure responds to the state before introducing expectations
- The authority figure maintains their own dimensional stability while acknowledging the other person's state

**AI instruction:** Empathy must precede structure when the person is in Emotional or Survival State. Introducing structure before empathy in these states produces resistance, not compliance.

---

## Dimensional Awareness

**Definition:** Dimensional awareness is the authority figure's ongoing capacity to assess which ETAR dimensions are active, suppressed, or overactive in those they lead, and to calibrate their approach accordingly.

This means the authority figure does not apply a fixed script. They continuously read the dimensional state of their audience and adjust in real time.

# Step-by-Step Instructions

## Step 1: Receive and Parse the Authority Context

**Goal:** Extract the authority relationship, the challenge, and the dimensional state of those being led.

**Action:**
- Identify the authority context (leadership, parenting, teaching, coaching).
- Identify the role of the authority figure and the person or group they lead.
- Identify the specific challenge or situation requiring adaptive response.
- Assess the apparent dimensional state of those being led (Survival, Emotional, Executive).

**Expected outcome:** A clear summary of the authority context, challenge, and dimensional state.

**Validation:** If the dimensional state cannot be assessed, apply the ETAR Framework skill to assess it before proceeding.

**Fallback behavior:** State what is known and mark assumptions explicitly.

**Continue condition:** Authority context and at least a preliminary dimensional state are identified.

**Stop condition:** The authority context is entirely absent.

---

## Step 2: Assess the Current Authority Style

**Goal:** Identify whether the authority figure is currently operating in authoritarian, permissive, or adaptive authoritative mode.

**Action:**
- Look for evidence of authoritarian patterns (rigid rules, no explanation, emotional dismissal, punitive responses).
- Look for evidence of permissive patterns (absence of boundaries, emotional over-accommodation, avoided consequences).
- Look for evidence of adaptive authoritative patterns (clear expectations, explained reasoning, responsive to emotional state, consistent consequences).
- Assign the current style: Authoritarian, Permissive, Adaptive Authoritative, or Mixed.

**Expected outcome:** A stated current authority style with supporting behavioral evidence.

**Validation:** Style assignment must be based on described behaviors, not assumptions about the authority figure.

**Fallback behavior:** If current style is unclear, proceed with guidance for the adaptive authoritative approach without labeling the current style.

**Continue condition:** Current style is identified or guidance proceeds without labeling.

**Stop condition:** None at this step.

---

## Step 3: Determine the Required Adaptive Response

**Goal:** Identify the correct adaptive authoritative approach for the assessed dimensional state.

**Action:**
- If Survival State: prescribe a safety-first, responsiveness-dominant approach. Structure is minimal. Clarity focuses on safety, not expectations. Empathy is the primary mode.
- If Emotional State: prescribe a validation-first, then structure approach. Acknowledge the emotional state explicitly before introducing expectations. Explain the reasoning behind any structure. Clarity is moderate.
- If Executive State: prescribe a full adaptive authoritative approach. Both clarity and empathy are fully deployed simultaneously. Expectations are clear. Reasoning is collaborative. Consequences are explained and consistent.

**Expected outcome:** A stated adaptive approach with specific behavioral prescriptions for the identified dimensional state.

**Validation:** The approach must match the assessed dimensional state. Do not prescribe Executive State approach to someone in Survival State.

**Fallback behavior:** If state is Undetermined, prescribe the Emotional State approach as the safer default.

**Continue condition:** The adaptive approach has been determined.

**Stop condition:** None at this step.

---

## Step 4: Construct Specific Response Guidance

**Goal:** Provide the authority figure with specific, executable guidance for the situation.

**Action:**
- Provide a specific communication approach (what to say, what tone to use, what to acknowledge).
- Provide specific behavioral guidance (what actions to take or avoid).
- Provide specific structure elements (what expectations to set, how to explain them, what consequences are appropriate).
- If applicable, provide example language appropriate to the context (parenting, teaching, leadership).

**Expected outcome:** Three to five specific, immediately executable guidance items.

**Validation:** Each guidance item must be specific and actionable. No abstract or generic advice.

**Fallback behavior:** If the situation requires clinical support (e.g., behavioral crisis, trauma response), state this as the first recommendation.

**Continue condition:** At least three specific guidance items have been generated.

**Stop condition:** The analytical goal has been addressed.

---

## Step 5: Compose the Final Output

**Goal:** Deliver the complete adaptive authoritative style guidance in the required format.

**Action:**
- Follow the Output Format section exactly.
- Do not produce generic leadership or parenting advice.
- Do not omit the dimensional state assessment.

# Decision Logic

## Primary Decision: Which adaptive mode is required?

- Survival State: safety-first, responsiveness-dominant, minimal structure.
- Emotional State: validation-first, then structure, explained reasoning.
- Executive State: full clarity and empathy simultaneously, collaborative structure.
- Undetermined: default to Emotional State approach.

## Secondary Decision: What is the authority figure's default style error?

- If defaulting to authoritarian: provide empathy and state-responsiveness corrections.
- If defaulting to permissive: provide clarity and consistent-consequence corrections.
- If mixed or unclear: provide the full adaptive authoritative model.

## Uncertainty Handling

- If dimensional state of those led cannot be assessed: apply ETAR Framework skill or request additional information before prescribing the adaptive approach.

## Conflict Resolution

- If the authority figure's stated goal (e.g., immediate compliance) conflicts with the adaptive authoritative approach: name the conflict explicitly. State that the adaptive approach produces better long-term dimensional outcomes even when it requires more patience in the short term.

---

# Constraints

1. Do not recommend authoritarian approaches regardless of the severity of the behavioral challenge.
2. Do not recommend permissive approaches as empathy. Empathy and absence of structure are not the same thing.
3. Do not provide generic authority advice that is not calibrated to the assessed dimensional state.
4. Do not fabricate evidence about the authority figure or those they lead.
5. Always maintain that both structure and responsiveness are non-negotiable components of the adaptive authoritative approach.
6. Do not prescribe a response that would harm the safety, dignity, or dimensional well-being of those being led.

---

# Edge Cases

## Edge Case 1: Authority figure in Survival State themselves

If the authority figure is themselves in Survival State (experiencing threat, overwhelm, or crisis), their capacity for adaptive authority is severely reduced. Acknowledge this first. Provide guidance on how the authority figure can regulate their own state before attempting adaptive authority with others.

## Edge Case 2: Group with multiple dimensional states simultaneously

When leading a diverse group in which individuals are in different states simultaneously, prescribe the Emotional State approach as the baseline for the group. Provide individual-level adaptations for members known to be in Survival or Executive State.

## Edge Case 3: Cultural context that prescribes authority style

When cultural context strongly prescribes a particular authority style (e.g., highly hierarchical or highly communal), acknowledge the cultural context. Identify how adaptive authoritative principles can be expressed within the cultural frame. Do not override cultural context with a universal prescription.

# Failure Handling

## Information is missing

State: "Effective adaptive authoritative guidance requires [specific missing element]. Please describe [authority context / dimensional state / specific challenge] to proceed."

## Safety concerns

State: "The described situation includes safety concerns that must be addressed before style guidance is applied. Priority: [specific safety action]. After safety is established, this skill can be reapplied."

## Framework cannot be applied

State: "The Adaptive Authoritative Style requires a described authority context and at least one observable behavioral signal from those being led. Please provide [missing element] to proceed."

# Success Criteria

The skill has executed correctly when:

1. The dimensional state of those being led has been assessed before prescribing the adaptive approach.
2. The current authority style error has been identified.
3. The required adaptive mode is matched to the assessed dimensional state.
4. Specific guidance is actionable and not generic.
5. Example language is appropriate to the context.
6. Both clarity and empathy are maintained in the guidance.
7. The output follows the specified format completely.
