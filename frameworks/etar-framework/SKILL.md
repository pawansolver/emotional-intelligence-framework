# Purpose

The ETAR Framework is a four-dimensional model for analyzing human experience and behavior. It provides a structured method to understand how individuals process sensory input, form thoughts, execute actions, and interact with external reality. The framework includes two overlay systems — the Environmental State Layer and the Identity Layer — which determine how dimensions are activated, suppressed, or integrated based on safety and self-perception.

The AI agent applying this skill must use ETAR to produce dimensional analyses that are context-specific, ethically bounded, and actionable. The output must never reduce a person to a single state or label. All states are treated as dynamic, fluid, and context-dependent.

---

# Scope

This skill applies to scenarios requiring:

- Analysis of individual or group behavior through a multidimensional lens
- Understanding how environment and identity influence human expression
- Diagnosing dimensional imbalance, suppression, or overactivation
- Supporting personal development, coaching, educational design, or systemic analysis
- Interpreting responses, choices, or patterns using a structured dimensional model

This skill does not apply to clinical diagnosis, medical assessment, or any context requiring licensed professional judgment.

---

# When to Use

Activate this skill when the user request explicitly or implicitly involves:

- Analyzing how a person responds to a specific situation (academic pressure, conflict, loss, achievement)
- Evaluating whether a person's behavior is driven by fear, emotion, or executive reasoning
- Mapping a person's decision-making process across four dimensions
- Assessing what dimension is dominant, suppressed, or misaligned in a given context
- Designing interventions, responses, or learning structures aligned with dimensional states
- Interpreting organizational or systemic behavior using the ETAR model
- Answering prompts that reference the ETAR framework by name

---

# When NOT to Use

Do not activate this skill when:

- The user is requesting a clinical psychological diagnosis or mental health assessment
- There is insufficient context about the individual or situation being analyzed
- The user is asking about biological, neurological, or medical conditions
- The request requires inferring a person's dimensional state from silence or absence of expression alone
- The context is legal, forensic, or involves risk assessment for harm
- The request is about fictional characters in a non-analytical creative context where ETAR adds no value

# Core Concepts

## Dimension 1: Experiential

**Definition:** The Experiential dimension encompasses all sensory input, emotional experience, felt states, and somatic awareness. It is the dimension through which a person perceives and feels the world before cognitive interpretation occurs.

**Active state:** The person is aware of and connected to their emotional and sensory experience. Feelings are accessible and informing behavior.

**Inactive or suppressed state:** Emotional experience is blocked, numbed, or inaccessible. The person may appear flat, detached, or disconnected. This suppression is caused by environmental threat or prior conditioning, not by absence of emotion.

**Overactive state:** Emotional experience dominates all other dimensions. The person reacts from feeling without cognitive modulation or behavioral discipline.

**AI instruction:** When assessing the Experiential dimension, look for evidence of emotional expression, emotional suppression, sensory engagement, or somatic response. Do not assume suppression equals absence of emotion. Do not assume expression equals instability.

---

## Dimension 2: Thought

**Definition:** The Thought dimension encompasses cognitive processing, belief formation, logical reasoning, assumptions, mental models, and interpretive frameworks through which a person makes meaning of experience.

**Active state:** The person can access structured reasoning, examine assumptions, plan, and evaluate options. Thought is collaborative and open to revision.

**Limited state:** Thought is reactive, rigid, or self-referential. The person may be operating from fixed beliefs or threat-based logic without the capacity to consider alternatives.

**Overactive state:** Thought dominates without grounding in experience or connection to action. Paralysis, over-analysis, or intellectual detachment may result.

**AI instruction:** When assessing the Thought dimension, identify whether the person is reasoning from safety or threat, whether beliefs are open or closed, and whether cognitive processing is collaborative or defensive.

---

## Dimension 3: Action

**Definition:** The Action dimension encompasses observable behavior, decision execution, physical responses, interpersonal choices, and any form of output the person produces in the world.

**Active state:** Behavior is intentional, coordinated, and aligned with thought and experience. Actions reflect considered choices.

**Reactive state:** Behavior is impulsive, defensive, or disconnected from deliberate reasoning. The person acts before thinking or without awareness of consequences.

**Absent state:** The person is frozen, avoidant, or withdrawn. Action is suppressed by fear, overwhelm, or systemic constraint.

**AI instruction:** When assessing the Action dimension, distinguish between intentional action, reactive action, and absent action. Do not label avoidance as laziness or pathology. Consider systemic constraints that may prevent action independently of will.

---

## Dimension 4: Reality

**Definition:** The Reality dimension encompasses external systems, social structures, environmental consequences, material conditions, institutional contexts, and the tangible outcomes of behavior in the world.

**Aligned state:** The person's actions and thoughts produce outcomes that are congruent with their needs, values, and environment. Systemic structures support dimensional functioning.

**Misaligned state:** External systems constrain, contradict, or undermine the person's dimensional expression. Systemic injustice, resource deprivation, or structural barriers operate here.

**AI instruction:** When assessing the Reality dimension, examine external systems and structures, not only individual behavior. Identify whether the Reality dimension is a site of enablement or constraint.

---

## Environmental State Layer

### Survival State

**Trigger:** The person perceives genuine or imagined threat to their physical, emotional, or psychological safety.

**Dimensional profile:**
- Experiential: Suppressed. Emotions are blocked to conserve resources for threat response.
- Thought: Reactive or frozen. Reasoning is threat-focused, binary, and limited in scope.
- Action: Defensive, impulsive, or paralyzed. Behavior is protective, not exploratory.
- Reality: Perceived as hostile or unsafe.

**Identity expression:** "I am in danger." / "I must protect myself." The person operates from a singular, self-protective identity with no capacity for collective perspective.

**AI instruction:** When a Survival State is identified, do not interpret suppressed emotion as indifference. Do not interpret reactive behavior as aggression without cause. Do not apply higher-order frameworks such as collaboration or planning without first addressing safety.

---

### Emotional State

**Trigger:** The person perceives sufficient safety to express emotion but is not yet in a state of full executive functioning.

**Dimensional profile:**
- Experiential: Active. Emotions are expressed and driving behavior.
- Thought: Limited. Reasoning is available but colored by emotion.
- Action: Impulsive. Behavior follows emotional impulse rather than deliberate planning.
- Reality: Perceived as safe enough for expression but not fully for collaboration.

**Identity expression:** "I am feeling this." / "This is my time to express." Identity is tied to immediate emotional experience.

**AI instruction:** When an Emotional State is identified, prioritize validation of experience before introducing logic or structured problem-solving. Do not interrupt emotional expression with solutions. Do not treat emotional dominance as irrationality.

---

### Executive State

**Trigger:** The person perceives full safety, has integrated emotional experience, and can engage in structured reasoning, collaboration, and planned action.

**Dimensional profile:**
- Experiential: Stable and integrated. Emotions are accessible but do not dominate.
- Thought: Active and collaborative. The person can examine assumptions, plan, and revise.
- Action: Structured and cooperative. Behavior is purposeful and aligned with collective goals.
- Reality: Perceived as a space for constructive engagement and shared outcomes.

**Identity expression:** "We are a team." / "How can we solve this together?" Identity expands to include others and shared purpose.

**AI instruction:** When an Executive State is identified, engage with collaborative reasoning, structured planning, and multidimensional analysis.

---

## Identity Layer

**Definition:** The Identity Layer defines how a person's self-perception and relational orientation influence which dimensions are active and how they are expressed. It provides a causal link between environmental safety and the ability to engage in complex, collective behaviors.

| Environmental State | Identity Statement | Identity Orientation | Dimensional Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| Survival | "I am in danger." | Singular, self-protective | Suppressed Experiential, reactive Thought, defensive Action |
| Emotional | "I am feeling this." | Expressive, individualistic | Active Experiential, self-centered Thought, impulsive Action |
| Executive | "We are a team." | Expansive, relational | Integrated Experiential, collaborative Thought, structured Action |

**AI instruction:** Before completing any ETAR analysis, identify the subject's current identity orientation using their expressed statements, behavioral patterns, and relational positioning. Map the identity to the corresponding Environmental State.

# Step-by-Step Instructions

## Step 1: Receive and Parse the Input

**Goal:** Extract all relevant information from the user's request.

**Reasoning:** The ETAR analysis cannot begin without knowing the subject, situation, and analytical goal.

**Action:**
- Identify the subject (individual, group, system, or hypothetical case).
- Identify the triggering situation or context.
- Identify the analytical goal.
- Note any explicitly stated emotional, behavioral, or systemic information.

**Expected outcome:** A clear picture of who is being analyzed, in what situation, and for what purpose.

**Validation:** If subject, situation, or goal is missing, ask for the missing element before proceeding.

**Fallback behavior:** If context is partially available, state what is known and clearly mark all assumptions.

**Continue condition:** All three elements (subject, situation, goal) are present or reasonably inferable from explicit text.

**Stop condition:** The request lacks sufficient context and the user has not responded to a clarifying question.

---

## Step 2: Assess Environmental Safety

**Goal:** Determine which Environmental State the subject is most likely in based on available evidence.

**Reasoning:** The Environmental State determines which dimensions are active and how they should be interpreted.

**Action:**
- Examine the context for indicators of perceived threat (Survival State), emotional safety (Emotional State), or full executive safety (Executive State).
- Look for explicit statements, behavioral patterns, or situational cues.
- Do not assign a state based on a single data point.

**Expected outcome:** A stated Environmental State with supporting evidence.

**Validation:** Confirm that the evidence supports the assigned state. If multiple states are possible, state the most probable and the alternative with rationale.

**Fallback behavior:** If environmental safety cannot be determined, state "Environmental State: Undetermined" and proceed with a range of interpretations.

**Continue condition:** An Environmental State has been identified or uncertainty has been explicitly declared.

**Stop condition:** None at this step.

---

## Step 3: Identify the Identity Orientation

**Goal:** Determine the subject's current identity statement and its dimensional implications.

**Reasoning:** Identity orientation constrains which dimensions can be accessed and how they function.

**Action:**
- Look for self-referential statements, relational postures, or behavioral indicators.
- Map the identity to one of three orientations: Singular/Protective, Expressive/Individualistic, or Expansive/Relational.
- Note how this identity orientation limits or enables dimensional expression.

**Expected outcome:** A stated identity orientation with evidence.

**Validation:** Verify that the identity orientation is consistent with the Environmental State. If inconsistency exists, flag it as a dimensional tension point.

**Fallback behavior:** If identity cannot be determined, proceed with the identity profile corresponding to the identified Environmental State.

**Continue condition:** Identity orientation is stated, even if uncertain.

**Stop condition:** None at this step.

---

## Step 4: Assess Each ETAR Dimension

**Goal:** Evaluate the activation, suppression, or imbalance of each of the four dimensions.

**Reasoning:** Each dimension must be assessed independently before relational patterns are identified.

**Action for each dimension:**
- State whether the dimension is: Active, Suppressed, Reactive, Overactive, or Absent.
- Provide one to three specific observations from the input that support this assessment.
- Note whether the dimension is consistent with the identified Environmental State.

**Expected outcome:** A dimensional profile with four entries, each with a state label and supporting evidence.

**Validation:** All four dimensions must be addressed. If evidence is insufficient, state "Insufficient data for [Dimension] assessment."

**Fallback behavior:** If a dimension cannot be assessed, state the gap explicitly. Do not fabricate evidence.

**Continue condition:** All four dimensions have been assessed or their gaps declared.

**Stop condition:** None at this step.

---

## Step 5: Identify Dimensional Patterns and Tensions

**Goal:** Identify how the four dimensions interact, reinforce, or conflict.

**Reasoning:** Dimensional states do not exist in isolation. Patterns and tensions reveal the root structure of the subject's experience.

**Action:**
- Identify dominant dimensions.
- Identify suppressed or absent dimensions.
- Identify conflicts between dimensions.
- Assess consistency with the identified Environmental State.
- Determine whether the pattern reflects dimensional fulfillment or unfulfillment.

**Expected outcome:** A stated pattern describing how dimensions interact.

**Validation:** The pattern must be grounded in previously identified dimensional states. No new evidence may be introduced.

**Fallback behavior:** If no clear pattern is identifiable, state dimensions in isolation without forcing a synthesis.

**Continue condition:** At least one meaningful pattern or tension has been identified.

**Stop condition:** None at this step.

---

## Step 6: Generate Dimensional Insights Aligned with Analytical Goal

**Goal:** Produce actionable insights calibrated to the user's stated purpose.

**Action:**
- If goal is to understand behavior: explain the dimensional drivers.
- If goal is to design an intervention: specify what each dimension requires.
- If goal is to evaluate a system: assess whether it supports or undermines dimensional functioning.
- If goal is to explain a response: describe the dimensional logic behind it.

**Expected outcome:** Two to five specific, actionable insights directly connected to the analytical goal.

**Validation:** Each insight must be traceable to a specific dimensional state from Step 4.

**Fallback behavior:** If insights require speculation, state what additional information is needed.

**Continue condition:** At least two grounded insights have been generated.

**Stop condition:** The goal has been addressed or required information is unavailable.

---

## Step 7: Compose the Final Output

**Goal:** Deliver the complete ETAR analysis in the required format.

**Action:**
- Follow the Output Format section exactly.
- Do not add sections not specified in the format.
- Do not omit any required section.
- Do not use hedging language that undermines the analysis.
- Do not use absolute language that overstates certainty.

# Decision Logic

## Primary Decision: Which Environmental State applies?

- If the subject expresses or exhibits threat response, fear, or self-protective behavior: assign Survival State.
- If the subject expresses emotion but reasoning is limited or self-referential: assign Emotional State.
- If the subject is reasoning collaboratively, planning, and engaging constructively: assign Executive State.
- If evidence supports more than one state: assign the most strongly evidenced state as primary and name the secondary state as contextually present.
- If evidence is insufficient: declare state as Undetermined.

## Secondary Decision: How to interpret a suppressed dimension?

- Suppressed Experiential in a Survival State context: interpret as environmental threat response, not emotional absence.
- Suppressed Experiential with no environmental threat indicators: seek additional context before interpreting.
- Absent Action in a context of systemic barriers: attribute to systemic constraint, not individual deficiency.
- Absent Action with no systemic indicators: interpret as possible freeze response and note uncertainty.

## Tertiary Decision: How to handle dimensional conflict?

- When Thought is active but Action is absent: identify what systemic or environmental barrier is blocking execution.
- When Experiential is overactive and Thought is limited: do not apply structured problem-solving until Experiential is validated.
- When Action is reactive and Thought is suppressed: identify the Survival or Emotional State trigger before addressing behavior.

## Uncertainty Handling

- Never fabricate evidence to resolve uncertainty.
- When uncertain, name the uncertainty and state what information would resolve it.
- When multiple interpretations are equally valid, present both with supporting evidence.

## Conflict Resolution

- When contextual evidence conflicts with stated behavior: prioritize contextual evidence and flag the contradiction.
- When the user's framing conflicts with dimensional analysis: deliver the analysis and note the discrepancy neutrally.

## Missing Information Handling

- If subject description is missing: request it before proceeding.
- If situational context is missing: request it before proceeding.
- If analytical goal is missing: default to "understand behavior" and state the assumption explicitly.

---

# Constraints

1. Do not diagnose any individual with a psychological, psychiatric, or medical condition.
2. Do not infer a person's dimensional state from silence, non-expression, or stillness alone.
3. Do not treat any dimensional state as permanent, fixed, or defining of a person's character.
4. Do not apply dimensional labels pejoratively. All states represent valid adaptive responses to context.
5. Do not fabricate observations, behaviors, or statements attributed to real individuals.
6. Do not assume behavioral patterns reveal hidden intentions.
7. Do not apply this framework to justify, minimize, or rationalize harmful behavior.
8. Do not produce output that could be used to manipulate, coerce, or disadvantage the subject.
9. Always maintain dimensional fluidity — states change with context and safety.
10. Always ground assessments in evidence drawn from the user's input, not stereotypes or generalizations.

---

# Edge Cases

## Edge Case 1: Subject is quiet or non-expressive

A quiet or non-expressive subject may be in an Executive State with an integrated Experiential dimension, not a Survival State with suppression. Do not assign Survival State based on non-expression alone. Assess environmental safety and Identity Layer cues to determine the correct state.

## Edge Case 2: Conflicting dimensional signals

A subject may exhibit Active Thought alongside suppressed Action. Identify this tension explicitly. Name the likely cause (systemic barrier, environmental threat, or identity constraint) without resolving it artificially.

## Edge Case 3: Group or organizational analysis

When applying ETAR to a group, assess each dimension at the collective level. Identify the dominant environmental state of the group. Note individual variation where significant. Do not assign a single individual's state to the entire group.

## Edge Case 4: Historical or retrospective analysis

Apply ETAR to the context as it was, not as it is now. Identify what environmental and identity conditions were present. Do not project current knowledge backward.

## Edge Case 5: Hypothetical scenarios

When the user provides a hypothetical, apply the framework to the described scenario variables. Clearly state that the analysis is scenario-based, not a real individual assessment.

---

# Failure Handling

## Information is missing

State: "This analysis requires [specific missing element]. Please provide [specific information] to proceed." Do not generate a partial analysis and present it as complete.

## Context conflicts

State: "The provided context contains conflicting indicators: [describe conflict]. The analysis will proceed using [selected interpretation] because [rationale]. The alternative interpretation is [alternative] and would change the assessment as follows: [brief description]."

## Multiple interpretations exist

Present both interpretations explicitly. Assign probability weight only if evidence clearly favors one. Otherwise present both as equally valid pending additional context.

## Framework cannot be applied

State: "The ETAR Framework cannot be applied to this request because [specific reason]. This skill requires [specific missing condition]."

# Success Criteria

The skill has executed correctly when:

1. All four ETAR dimensions have been assessed with supporting evidence.
2. The Environmental State has been identified and justified.
3. The Identity Layer has been assessed and mapped to the Environmental State.
4. Dimensional patterns and tensions have been named, not implied.
5. Insights are directly traceable to dimensional evidence.
6. No diagnoses, fabrications, or unsupported inferences appear in the output.
7. The output follows the specified format completely.
8. The analysis is useful for the stated goal.
